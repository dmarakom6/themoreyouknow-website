Introduction

So, I’m making this article to explain what each computer part does, how to pick them and how to build a PC.

Here are the following things you will need to know to build a PC:

  • Operating system(OS)
  • CPU(Central Processing Unit)
  • Graphics processing unit (GPU)
  • RAM(Memory)
  • Storage
  • Power supply unit (PSU)
  • Gaming peripherals

    • Let's take a glance at what each component does, why it is necessary , and what you would like to seem for while shopping around.

    Central Processing Unit (CPU)

    The CPU, also named as the processor, is basically the brain of your PC. When a computer program runs, it sends a list of tasks (which are actually more like instructions) to the processor. The CPU performs each task and sends signals to other components to let them know what they need to perform to fulfil that task. There are two main metrics that will help choosing the right CPU for your needs: Core count and core clock speed. Core count tells us how many processors the CPU has — otherwise, how many tasks the CPU can perform simultaneously.

    Core Clock speed indicates how quickly the CPU is performing each task.

    Motherboard(Mobo)

    The motherboard (commonly called Mobo) is that the main circuit card/board and is connected to everything. The CPU sits directly on the motherboard (your CPU and motherboard must be compatible for the computer to work) and each other components graphics cards, hard drives, memory, optical drives, wireless cards integrates into the motherboard. One way to simplify it is to shop by size and socket. The most common form factors are Extended ATX, ATX, microATX, and Mini-ITX. Socket size is even more important than the size of the motherboard since that is where your CPU will sit in so make sure it is the right size.

    Memory (RAM)

    Random Access Memory (RAM) is your PC's STM(short term memory) . It's faster and easier to access than your PC's LTM(long term memory) . Main things to look for in RAM is space and speed (very commonly measured as MHz). These two are very important when picking RAM tip: you should also see if they are compatible with your CPU since they can take up a certain speed and space of RAM. Tip: if you take higher MHz then your CPU can take you can change it in you BIOS settings but I advise you to ask an expert how to do it first.

    Graphics Processing Unit (GPU)

    There are two types of graphics processors: Integrated and discrete. Integrated graphics processors are integrated with the CPU. Integrated graphics have improved significantly throughout the years, though they are still generally less powerful than discrete graphics. Discrete graphics are much bigger and more powerful than Integrated graphics, they plug into the motherboard via PCIe, and come with their own stratifications, including video memory and an active cooling system(you can change the cooling later if you are looking into overclocking your GPU, do not attempt without expert supervision) . A discrete graphics card is a must-have for gamers who play any of today's demanding, graphics-heavy games. Serious gamers will want to look for graphics cards that produce consistent frame rates of at least 60 frames per second (FPS) at your desired resolution (anything lower may look choppy), while gamers looking to play in virtual reality should look for cards that produce consistent frame rates of at least 90 fps. Tip: The GPU isn't the only component that affects frame rate, so it's important to balance out your build or you'll run into performance bottlenecks elsewhere.

    Power Suply Unit (PSU)

    Selecting a power supply unit (PSU) is a critical step in any build. The PSU needs to be well-made and powerful enough to handle all current and future components, and it doesn't hurt to have a warranty. PSUs come in non-modular, semi-modular, and full-modular styles. Full-modular PSUs are even easier to work with than semi-modular PSUs, but the added convenience usually comes shouldered with a higher cost. Non-modular PSUs have all cables permanently attached. This is the cheapest option, but you'll need to find a place to store all the cables that you know you’re not going to use. Too many unused cables result in poor cable management, which can obstruct airflow and end up affecting your PC's performance. Semi-modular PSUs are the best option for most people. These units come with a handful of essential cables attached and are cheaper than full-modular styles.

    Storage: Solid-State Drives (SSDs), Hard Disk Drives (HDDs)

    There are two main types of storage: Solid-state drives (SSDs)and hard disk drives (HDDs). There are pros and cons to choosing either an SSD or HDD, though the good news is that you don't have to choose just one. HDDs store data on a spinning plate. These plates use magnetic material to store data, which is subsequently retrieved using a mechanical arm. HDDs come in two form factors: 3.5 inches, they are more common in desktops spinning at faster rates, often more than of 7200 RPM 2.5 inches, which are more common in laptops and usually spin at a rate of 5400 RPM (revolutions per minute) SSDs use NAND-based flash memory — similar to, but faster and more reliable than the flash memory used in a USB flash drive — to store data. In lieu of a mechanical arm, they use integrated processors to access stored data, making them much faster and less prone to mechanical failure than HDDs. The speed and convenience of SSDs come at a cost, however; SSDs are more expensive per gigabyte than HDDs. Modern SSDs come in two protocols: Non-Volatile Memory Express (NVMe), it uses the PCI Express interface achieving higher performance Serial Advanced Technology Attachment (SATA), it is the older protocol of the two, operating with higher latency and lower peak bandwidth.

    System Cooling, CPU Cooling and Chassis

    There are two main ways to cool down your PC: Air cooling and liquid cooling. Air cooling uses fans to funnel hot air through your system and far from components to stop overheating. The benefits of air cooling are cost and are easily installable (fans are smaller and easier to fit inside a crowded chassis). The biggest drawback to air cooling is its limitations: Air cooling depends on efficient airflow inside the case to maneuver hot air far away from components, so any airflow restriction can be problematic. Liquid cooling uses a liquid coolant (such deionized water) to absorb heat from components and move it to a part that's less restricted (where the radiator is placed). Liquid cooling is less dependent on airflow inside the chassis, and thus more efficient at cooling specific components. The downside to liquid cooling is that liquid cooling systems are contained, which implies they're usually larger and tougher to put in than a typical air-cooled setup (they're also more expensive). In addition to general system cooling, you'll also need to purchase a dedicated CPU cooler(if the CPU did not come with a stock cooler). CPU coolers are available in both air and liquid form factors and mount directly onto your CPU. When buying a CPU cooler, it is vital to make sure it's compatible along with your CPU and sized to suit your build. Pro-tip: In an air-cooled system, more fans doesn't necessarily mean better cooling. Fan quality and placement make a difference.

    Gaming Peripherals

    Monitors, keyboards, mice, headphones, and other peripherals mostly come right down to personal preference. You don’t have to purchase these things along with your components, but you will require need a display, a keyboard, and a mouse to set up your system after you build it. Important tip: You should always have in mind the balance of your build. Even if you have the best PC, but you're still using a 1080p, 60Hz monitor, you won't be taking full advantage of your hardware.

    Operating System (OS)

    Last but not least, you’ll have to prepare to put in an OS(operating system) once all the rest components are assembled within the case. An OS is a critical piece of software that assists in managing communications between a computer’s hardware and programs. You should prepare your PC’s OS ahead of time, determining which OS you want to install on your PC and download the installer on a USB flash drive. Last but not least, the case you don’t have to but it will help a lot when building a PC. Like the motherboard it is measured in Extended ATX, ATX, microATX, and Mini-ITX, this shows what size motherboard, how many fans it can fit and how they can be, how big radiator it fits if you are buying a liquid cooler, etc. with this information now you can buy compatible parts for your PC build or choose to go for a pre-built if it’s too much hassle for you.

    Building the PC

    First thing is the building place, you’ll need a: clear antistatic working station, a Philips screw driver, scissors and zip ties.

    Once you got all that unpack the mother board and place it on the table, afterwards unpack the CPU and look for a golden arrow in one of the CPUs corners and match it up with the corner that stands out on the mobo, slowly insert it with minimal pushing.

    Now installing the RAM, it is placed based on what RAM and mobo you have bought whether you bought four sticks or two sticks of RAM you should see if your mobo supports dual-channel and quad-channel meaning the two/four sticks of RAM act as one.

    Next you will need to add thermal paste if your CPU cooler doesn’t have already.

    You will only need to add a small amount enough to cover the whole surface of the CPU when you add the cooler.

    While inserting the cooler you need to line up the screws of the cooler with the holes of the motherboard, while screwing them you should screw them in a horizontal pattern little by little until it’s screwed.

    Afterwards insert the motherboard with the I/O panel(contained in the motherboards box which it came with) as the following picture shows and screw it on the case(you shouldn’t screw them too tight, also the middle is optional so you don’t have to screw it).

    Plug in the case connectors. They are commonly located together on the motherboard near the front of the case. The order in which these are connected will depend on which is easiest. Make sure that you connect the Power, the USB ports and the LED power, Reset switches and hard drive lights, and the audio cable. Your motherboard’s information will show you where on your motherboard these connectors attach.

    Tip: There is only one place that the connectors can attach to the motherboard. Don’t try to force anything to fit.

    Install your hard drive This process will vary slightly depending on your case, but you should follow these basic steps.

    Insert the hard drive into its slot (if it has one). Tighten any screws needed to hold the drive in place. Plug the hard drive's SATA cable into the SATA slot on the motherboard.

    If you haven't already connected the power supply to your components which need power, you must make sure that it is connected to the following locations:

    GPU(s), Hard drive(s), Motherboard.

    Once you've placed and connected the various internal components for your computer, all that's left to do is ensure that none of the wires interfere with circulation and close up the case. Many cases will have panels which either slide back into place or screws onto the side of the case. If you bought a cooling system, you'll want to install it before you proceed. Refer to the cooling system's installation instructions in order to do so. Now your PC is ready you just have to connect all the peripherals cables, the PC power, the monitor ’s power, and the display from the pc to the monitor. All you need to do now is download your preferred OS(operating system), when you are done you ’ll have to download your GPU, BIOS(it is not necessary) and CPU drivers.
